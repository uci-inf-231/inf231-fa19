Project

A course project will be a major component of your work. It will require hands-on experience with Human-Computer Interaction, while remaining open to different possibilities.

Your course project must have three components, aligning with three common phases in the human centered design process:

  1. A formative component, in which you must understand the needs of the people you are designing for.
  2. A design component, in which you must design a prototype to meet those needs.
  3. An evaluative component, in which you must evaluate that prototype.

We expect most students will do projects in pairs. We will consider groups of three, and very strongly discourage individuals. Working in groups is a necessary part of both research and practice, and is also important for the logistics of this course.

Some sample project ideas will be gathered ahead of time on Slack. You can also use that discussion to identify potential project partners and post your own ideas.

Project Proposal

Your proposal will define your initial plan for this project. You can and should update this plan based on feedback, progress, and findings. But the proposal needs to demonstrate a robust initial plan for your project.

Projects must have a formative, design, and evaluative component. It might be helpful to read the subsequent sections (formative, design, evaluative) to get a sense of whether your project idea will fit the course's requirements.

You are welcome and even encouraged to align your project with your research, other course projects, or other goals outside this course. However, it is critical to define what you will accomplish in the course project. The course project must stand on its own.

Formative Component

In the formative component, you must conduct some sort of activity to understand the needs of the people you are designing for. Since choice of formative methods depend on access and population needs, we are not requiring one specific method. Some example methods include:

  • Surveys
  • Contextual Inquiry
  • Interviews
  • Focus Groups

The course staff is happy to offer guidance in choosing an appropriate formative method. As you will need to evaluate your design with your target population, we encourage methods which involve directly engaging with potential users. However, formative methods sometimes take the form of observing people's discussion via public records (e.g., social media activity, forum discussions, product and app reviews). These methods may be used if they are more appropriate to understand the pain point outlined in the project proposal.

The formative assessment does not need to be at the rigor required for a research publication, but must be enough to start to suggest some design directions. For example, it may be sufficient to survey 20-30 people (publications often survey hundreds), conduct inquiries or interviews with 2-3 people (10-30 is typical), or conduct a single focus group with a 3-4 people (5 or more small groups is common).

Design Component

In the design component, you must develop some sort of prototype of your design. Unless otherwise discussed with the course staff, your prototype must be digital (e.g., an app, a website, a virtual reality experience). Prototypes need not be feature-complete, but should support at least two tasks motivated by the formative assessments.

First, define the two (or more) tasks your design will support. Aim to align the task with real-world actions (e.g., "schedule a meeting from noon to 3pm") rather than interface actions (e.g., "use the date selections to pick 1pm and 3pm as the start and end times and push the 'schedule meeting' button"). The description should clarify what constitutes successful completion of the task.

In prototyping, you must first prototype on paper, showing at least a few sketches for screens in support of the two tasks. You must then translate your design to a higher-fidelity (e.g., wireframe, mockup, clickable/interactive, implemented) and incorporate feedback from your evaluation. In general, we recommend lower-fidelity designs to enable feedback to better match the design, but we acknowledge that in-progress projects may have mockup or implementation work already underway. You may find it useful to conduct a heuristic evaluation or usability test of your low-fidelity prototype, but neither is required at this stage.

We will not require any specific tools for prototyping, but some recommendations include:

  • Balsamiq
  • Axure
  • Sketch
  • Adobe XD

We acknowledge that these tools vary in price, but some are free (Adobe XD) and many have trial editions to allow for exploration.

Evaluative Component

In the evaluative component, you must take your design back to your your target population for feedback. You must first make any changes to your prototype in response to the in-class design critique. Make these changes prior to starting evaluation; otherwise, you may get redundant feedback!

Like in the formative component, we will not specify an evaluative method. Pick an appropriate method based on the state of the prototype and what feedback would be most useful. We strongly encourage methods which allow for conversation (e.g., interviews) over methods which only allow for one-directional feedback (e.g., surveys). Evaluations may focus only on usability (e.g., heuristic evaluation) or a mixture of usability and utility.

Similar to the formative component, the evaluative assessment does not need to be at the rigor required for a research publication. For example, it is sufficient to interview with 2-3 people.

Document

Prepare a roughly 3-page document addressing the following points:

  • Re-state the pain point you are looking to address and include a few screenshots of your prototype.
  • Describe any changes you made to your design in response to the critique or further reflection.
  • Explain your evaluation method and why you selected that method. Describe your population sample.
  • Describe what you learned from your evaluative assessment and how could impact another phase of design or implementation. Aim for at least three takeaways.

Feel free to include any materials relevant to your progress as an appendix (e.g., does not count toward the 2 page limit). Example materials include an interview protocol or notes from interviews.

Final Poster

"Poster" is a bit of a misnomer for this deliverable; we will not create physical posters because they're expensive to print and wasteful for use in a single event. Instead, create a slide deck which summarizes the core contributions of your project and your design. The slide deck should still serve the role similar to a poster: a visual aide which a passerby at a coffee break can glance at for a minute or two to get a gist of your project's goal and what you accomplished. Aim for 4-6 slides which explain your project's goals and each stage of the design. Since you may be talking with someone about your project, set up your slides to auto-advance every ~15 seconds or so and loop back to the beginning on completion.

Each team will have a monitor next to their table; you will present from your own laptop. Consider bringing a second laptop so you can show your designs to an interested persion while your slide deck continues presenting. We can provide some monitors, but if your work in DBH and have a monitor which is easy to move, please bring it to the poster session. We can provide a cart to help transport monitors.

Final Report

Prepare a final report that is 4 to 10 pages in length, excluding references.

Your final report should be presented the general structure of a research paper:

  • Introduction and Motivation
  • Overview of Process
  • Description of Methods
  • Presentation of Results
  • Discussion / Future Work / Conclusion
  • References, if applicable

Your prior reports should provide a good starting point for preparing this report. Write according to the content you have. Be appropriate thorough and precise in your presentation, but do not needlessly pad your text.

Use the SIGCHI Proceedings Format: https://chi2020.acm.org/authors/chi-proceedings-format/. You may use either Word or LaTeX to prepare your paper. Do not spend substantial time trying to correct formatting inconsistencies, just do your best.

Additional guidance regarding effective paper writing can be found in:

Jacob O. Wobbrock. Catchy Titles Are Good: But Avoid Being Cute. Writing Advice, White Paper, 2015.
